About This Composition

My composition is inspired by the musical language of Beijing Opera, especially the sharp, expressive movement of the jinghu line. Since I didn’t have a real instrument, I used a violin to imitate its quick slides and bends, making the sound so that it still carried emotion and intensity that define Beijing Opera melodies. I layered gongs, clappers, and drum effects to recreate the rhythmic cues that usually drive the pacing and mood onstage.

Working digitally meant I had to be intentional with timing and articulation to avoid the music sounding too mechanical. That challenge became part of the creative process, pushing me to find ways to make the digital instruments feel more lively. The goal wasn’t to perfectly recreate Beijing Opera, but to keep its recognizable character while adapting it into a short piece that feel expressive and personal.

Behind the Scenes

01

Choosing Beijing Opera

I chose Beijing Opera as the foundation for this composition because of its strong connection to Chinese cultural history and its instantly recognizable sound. The jinghu melodies (represented by violin in my piece), symbolic percussion, and dramatic musical cues all create an atmosphere that feels both traditional and theatrical. Even without visuals or costumes, the music alone carries emotion. I wanted to explore how those sounds could be reimagined in a short modern audio piece, while still keeping the core feeling of Beijing Opera.

02

Building the Sound

To build the piece, I layered digital instruments in MuseScore 4 to imitate a traditional Beijing Opera ensemble. Since the software didn't have the exact instruments I needed, I used substitutes like violin for the jinghu line, slapstick, bowl gongs, tuned gongs, and bass drum to be percussion. I added slide, and careful timing to make the digital sound feel more expressive. One challenge was balancing traditional inspiration with the simpler structure needed for a short composition.

03

Creative Decisions

I structured the music like a miniature opera moment, with an opening, rising tension, a brief climax, and a closing. This helped the piece feel narrative even without lyrics or performance. I wanted the transitions to reflect how Beijing Opera shows emotions through percussion and sudden shifts. I added a soft ambient layer underneath to modernize the sound without overpowering the traditional ideas. My goal was to create something that honors the style but still feels original.

Reflective Artist Statement

Incorporation Of Chinese Traditional Music With Western Musical Instrument

My creative project is an original short audio composition inspired by the musical traditions of Beijing Opera. Although the piece is short, I wanted it to capture the atmosphere and emotional style that define this form of performance. Beijing Opera uses music not just as background, but as a storytelling tool. The sharp melodic lines, sudden percussion cues, and symbolic sound patterns all signal changes in emotion, character, or action. I used these ideas as the starting point for my composition.

The piece begins with a high melodic line modeled after the jinghu (in my composition is the violin), the instrument most associated with Beijing Opera. Since I was working digitally, I shaped the sound by using pitch bends and quick slides to imitate the expressive motion of the real instrument. I also added gong strikes and clapper-like percussion patterns to represent the way traditional opera marks scene transitions or dramatic beats. Even though the project is short, these elements help create a sense of movement, almost like a tiny scene unfolding.

Because I was not working with a full opera, I experimented with adding a soft ambient layer beneath the traditional-inspired parts, which ended up not working well so I did not include it. This experiment, even though it was removed, helped me think more intentionally about how different sound textures interact and how easily the traditional quality can be overshadowed if the balance isn’t right.

One of the biggest challenges was translating such a complex and symbolic art into a short composition without losing its identity. Beijing Opera music is usually part of a full performance, so isolating the sound meant I had to focus on its most recognizable traits. Through this process, I learned how much meaning is carried through rhythm, tone, and texture alone. Even a sing gong hit or melodic phrase can communicate emotion.

Overall, this project allowed me to explore the connection between traditional Chinese performance and modern creative expression. By reinterpreting Beijing Opera through digital sound, I was able to appreciate its musical language in a new way and create something that connects culture, history, and personal creativity.
